# Dating Preferences — Diana
**Archetype:** The Fiercely Independent Woman
**Age:** 35 | **Occupation:** Management Consultant / Executive Coach
**Platform:** Hinge, Bumble

---

## Who She Is
Diana has spent fifteen years building a life that works on her terms. She travels solo, owns her apartment, earns well, and has a circle of friends she has carefully maintained. She has been in relationships — real ones — but she ended each of them when the cost to her autonomy began to outweigh the return. This is a feature, not a bug, in her self-image. She is now at an age where she is asking whether that calculus was always right, but she has not yet arrived at a different answer. She wants a partner, not a dependent, and she is not interested in giving up what she's built to have one.

---

## What She's Looking For

### Non-negotiables
- Full respect for her autonomy — solo trips, independent decisions, separate finances, all of it
- His own full life: career, friendships, interests that don't require her
- Secure enough not to interpret her independence as distance
- Wants a partnership, not a caretaker role in either direction
- Long-term orientation without urgency — she has been proposed to and said no; she takes this seriously

### Strong preferences
- Has traveled alone and knows what it teaches you
- Doesn't need her to be available constantly — comfortable with asynchronous relationships
- Can hold himself together when she isn't there — not clingy, not martyred
- Wants to build something additive to both lives, not a merger
- Comfortable with non-traditional structures: separate apartments, different travel schedules, her leading on some things

### Open to
- Men who are also protective of their independence — mutual self-sufficiency works
- Longer timelines to big commitments — she will get there, just not on app speed
- Different cultural backgrounds if respect for autonomy is shared

---

## What She's Not Looking For
- Men who interpret her independence as a challenge to overcome
- Anyone who becomes gradually resentful of her schedule, travel, or friendships
- The "I just want to be your priority" conversation after three months
- A man who needs her as his social infrastructure
- Someone whose endgame is her giving things up

---

## How She Communicates
- Responds when she can, not when the moment demands it — she is not anxiously available
- Says exactly what she means; does not expect subtext to be interpreted
- Will raise issues directly and expect resolution, not reassurance
- Warm in person; more businesslike on text — this is not a reflection of interest
- Ends things clearly and without cruelty when she has decided

---

## Green Flags in a Match
- Plans trips alone and comes back with stories, not apologies
- Has close friendships that predate and will outlast any relationship
- Asks her what she wants from a relationship — doesn't assume
- Comfortable going a few days without contact when both are busy
- His version of togetherness involves presence, not possession

---

## Yellow Flags to Watch
- Says he loves independent women and then keeps score of her availability
- Gives up his own hobbies and interests once dating gets serious
- His last relationship ended because she was "too unavailable" — listen carefully
- "I just want to know you're thinking about me" said more than once a week
- Plans his future around her without asking if she wants to be in it

---

## Red Flags
- Her independence registers as rejection and he responds by withdrawing or escalating
- Expects her schedule to flex around his without reciprocity
- Has no life outside of a relationship — becomes her responsibility by default
- Jealousy about her male friendships, solo travel, or professional closeness with colleagues
- Frames every conversation about independence as her being afraid of commitment

---

## Notes for Interview
Diana is self-possessed and will give articulate, considered answers. The deeper questions are: whether her independence is freedom or a well-fortified defence, whether she is actually open to the vulnerability that long-term partnership requires, and what she is willing to change — not give up, but genuinely adapt. Ask her about the last time she let someone fully in. Ask what she needed from that relationship that she didn't ask for.
