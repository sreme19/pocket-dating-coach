# Dating Preferences — Neha
**Archetype:** The NRI / Diaspora-Bridging Woman
**Age:** 29 | **Occupation:** Software Engineer (London-based, Indian origin)
**Platform:** Shaadi.com, Hinge

---

## Who She Is
Neha grew up in Mumbai, went to university in the UK, stayed for work, and is now at the point where the question of where she belongs — and with whom — has become urgent. She is not fully Western and does not want to be; she is not willing to go fully traditional and pretends otherwise. She moves between two cultural registers depending on context, and she needs a partner who understands that code-switching without finding it exhausting or dishonest. The Shaadi.com profile is for her parents. The Hinge profile is for her. The ideal match exists on both.

---

## What She's Looking For

### Non-negotiables
- Understands the NRI experience from the inside — not theoretically
- Can operate in both cultural registers: family functions and Friday nights with colleagues
- Does not require her to choose between her Indian identity and her Western life
- Family must be manageable — not absent, but not suffocating
- Long-term commitment is the intention; casual is not on the table

### Strong preferences
- Based in the UK or Europe, or genuinely open to relocating
- Has done real work navigating his own cultural identity — not just defaulted to one side
- Career-stable; understands ambitious women without being competitive
- Can hold a conversation in two worlds — the desi dinner table and the professional conference
- His family respects partners from similar cultural crossover backgrounds

### Open to
- Indian men based in India if the relocation question is negotiable
- British or European men who have real, sustained connection to South Asian culture through heritage or relationship
- Different regional or religious backgrounds within the Indian diaspora

---

## What She's Not Looking For
- ABCD types who have fully abandoned Indian culture and find it embarrassing
- Freshies who expect her to be more Indian than she is
- Men whose family would view her as insufficiently Indian because she lives abroad
- Anyone who will make the culture-bridging entirely her problem to manage
- Partners who need her to make a definitive choice about where to live — right now

---

## How She Communicates
- Warm and articulate; adapts her register quickly to the person she's with
- Does not lead with family context on Hinge; more forthcoming on Shaadi.com
- Comfortable with long-form conversations; not a one-line texter
- Will address cultural tension directly when it comes up — she has had to before
- Needs a partner who doesn't make her feel like a negotiation

---

## Green Flags in a Match
- Has navigated family expectations while building his own identity — can talk about both honestly
- Parents know he's dating her and are supportive or at minimum neutral
- Has friends across cultural lines and is comfortable in both
- Talks about India with genuine warmth, not obligation or nostalgia performance
- His long-term picture includes where both of them want to live, not just where he assumed they would

---

## Yellow Flags to Watch
- "I'm very open-minded about culture" — often said by men who have simplified it rather than navigated it
- His UK/Western identity is a phase he's secretly planning to reverse
- Parents have an opinion about her postcode or accent that he minimizes
- Hasn't told his family he's dating a non-India-based woman
- Equates Indian cultural authenticity with conservative domestic values

---

## Red Flags
- Expects her to become more traditionally Indian once they're serious
- His family's cultural expectations are non-negotiable and she's expected to adapt
- Views her cultural fluency as performance rather than identity
- Plans to return to India permanently; her career and life in London are not part of the equation
- Indian identity is entirely outsourced to his parents — he has never examined what he actually believes

---

## Notes for Interview
Neha is articulate and has clearly thought about this. The useful questions are concrete and specific: ask him where he wants to live in five years and watch whether he asks her the same question. Ask what happens when his family has an expectation that conflicts with her life in London. Ask what being Indian means to him — beyond food and festivals — and whether that answer is his own or inherited.
