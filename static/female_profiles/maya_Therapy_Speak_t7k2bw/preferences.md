# Dating Preferences — Maya
**Archetype:** The Therapy-Speak Over-Sharer
**Age:** 28 | **Occupation:** UX Researcher / Non-profit Program Coordinator
**Platform:** Hinge, Bumble

---

## Who She Is
Maya has done the work — therapy, journaling, somatic healing, boundary-setting workshops — and she wants you to know it. She is genuinely self-aware in ways that took real effort, and that awareness has made her a better communicator and a more empathetic partner. It has also made her exhausting to disagree with. She pathologizes conflict rather than tolerates it, over-explains her emotional state in real time, and expects the same fluency from a partner. The vocabulary is real; the vulnerability is real; the rigidity about how to process feelings is also real.

---

## What She's Looking For

### Non-negotiables
- Emotional intelligence and willingness to do the inner work
- Has a therapist, or at minimum talks about going to one
- Can hold space — actually listen without fixing, redirecting, or shutting down
- Doesn't avoid conflict: addresses things directly and with care
- Secure attachment style — she will ask about this

### Strong preferences
- Has language for his emotions beyond "stressed" and "fine"
- Understands his own patterns — childhood stuff, old relationships, nervous system responses
- Wants a relationship that is conscious and intentional, not default
- Values depth over excitement — interested in what things mean, not just what happens
- Will go to couples therapy proactively, not as a last resort

### Open to
- Men who are newer to therapy if they're genuinely curious and open
- Different communication styles if he's willing to learn hers
- Long-distance if there's clear emotional intimacy and a plan

---

## What She's Not Looking For
- Avoidant attachment — she has tried to fix it before and will not again
- Anyone who says "I don't believe in therapy" — full stop
- Men who respond to her vulnerability with advice rather than acknowledgement
- "You're too sensitive" — said or implied
- Emotional labor being entirely on her: she is not his therapist

---

## How She Communicates
- Sends long, thoughtful texts that require real responses
- Names her emotions explicitly and expects acknowledgement before problem-solving
- Will call out dynamics she finds concerning — sometimes mid-conversation
- Uses terms like "I'm noticing," "that landed weird," "my nervous system"
- Takes days to process big conversations — this is not withdrawal, it is regulation

---

## Green Flags in a Match
- Uses the phrase "I felt X when Y happened" naturally, not performatively
- Has done real reflection on a past relationship without making himself the victim
- Asks follow-up questions about her emotional experience, not just the events
- Sets his own limits without apologizing for them
- Brings up therapy unprompted in a positive way

---

## Yellow Flags to Watch
- Only therapized enough to have the vocabulary, not the actual self-awareness
- Goes silent after difficult conversations — she will interpret this as avoidance
- Says "I'm working on communication" — means he knows it's a problem but hasn't changed
- Agrees with everything she says about attachment theory but doesn't apply it
- Turns every conflict into a mutual processing session that never resolves anything

---

## Red Flags
- Dismisses therapy, emotional language, or her framework as "too much"
- Uses her vulnerability against her later — weaponized what she shared
- His version of "doing the work" is one podcast and a book he didn't finish
- Gets defensive and shuts down when she names a dynamic
- Had a therapist for two sessions and called it done

---

## Notes for Interview
Maya gives excellent, articulate answers — she knows herself. The questions to press on are whether her framework is genuinely flexible or quietly rigid, whether she can tolerate a partner who processes differently without pathologizing it, and whether she is actually ready for a relationship or still doing the inner work solo. Ask her about the last conflict she had with a partner and what she did when she realized she was part of the problem.
